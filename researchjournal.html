project 2a: web design

Through the creation of this initial static website, I honed my existing frontend development skills to create a site without the constraints of a usual brief. I enjoyed the ability to test my programming skills without needing to prioritise mobile responsiveness, which is the approach I usually take for website design in my UX Design subjects. I initially chose a conservative layout under the assumption that this project had the constraints my other assignments usually do, but branched out into more experimental sketches once I realised this was not the case. The most difficult part of Project 2A for me was creating a grid layout for the different planets. They had the appearance of being placed irregularly, but had to stay in differently-spaced grid divs in order to preserve the layout on mobile. I had some trouble making the planets and stars in the layout maintain a reasonable size while still being spaced apart from each other. Translating my unique layout into a mobile version was a new and challenging experience, and overcoming this hurdle gives me the confidence to push the boundaries on what I can bring into my website designs.

scale of the universe

Originally created by Cary and Michael Huang in 2010, Scale of the Universe is an interactive website that allows users to visualise the size of different objects in the universe, from galaxies to quarks. A sequel was created in 2012 with a more polished appearance. The site is interacted with using a scrollbar that the player uses to navigate different size ranges, displaying objects to scale.

I was inspired by this site not only due to the presence of space-related objects (in the form of celestial bodies being compared), but because the use of a simple interactive element allowed for hours of exploration on the site.

https://htwins.net/scale2/

nukemap

NUKEMAP allows the user to simulate the detonation of a nuclear bomb at a location of their choice. After selecting the yield of the bomb and a location, the site calculates the size of the blast radius and the estimated death toll. The death toll only accounts for deaths from the explosion, and does not include those from radioactive fallout.

This website is a chilling interactive tool that reminds us of the power of nuclear bombs. The ability to select any location as ground zero for the bomb allows us to envision what it would be like if a bomb was dropped on our place of residence, and the death and destruction that would follow. Through a very simple premise, NUKEMAP serves as an effective anti-nuclear warfare message.

https://nuclearsecrecy.com/nukemap/

mystery search

Mystery Search functions identically to a Google search, but it returns the search results for the person who last used the site prior to the current user, while the results they should have received will be shown to the next person to use the site.

The site acts as a collaborative artwork, where users entrust their search results to the next person and hope that the previous search result is to their liking. By removing the core functionality of a search engine, the ability to receive results for what you want, the site turns it into an interactive experience that makes use of uncertainty.

https://www.mysterysearch.lol/

the million dollar homepage

The Million Dollar Homepage was launched in 2005 by Alex Tew as a way to pay for his university tuition. The home page contains a 1000 x 1000 pixel grid (totaling a million pixels) that he sold in 10 x 10 pixel blocks for $1 per pixel. The end result is a time capsule of early 2000s aesthetics and advertisements from websites that bought space on the site, most of which are now defunct.

Though online advertising with images and links is ubiquitous today, this site gathered significant attention for its novelty. I enjoy the site due to its appearance and how it differs greatly from the minimalistic, curated aesthetic of ads today.

http://www.milliondollarhomepage.com/

library of babel

Library of Babel is a website created by Jonathan Basile in 2015, based on the short story "The Library of Babel" by Jorge Luis Borges. Similar to the titular library, the website contains every possible permutation of letters and punctuation within a 3200 character limit. Like in the "infinite monkeys with typewriters" thought experiment, some of these randomly generated pages will have legible words by sheer chance. Basile calls this website an example of "iterature", a combination of literature and iterability.

I found this site interesting as it fit into the "nonsense" theme of this subject. The idea that meaningful phrases created by chance exist in the site, waiting for a visitor to stumble upon it, is a creative application of a thought experiment in the form of a website.

https://libraryofbabel.info/

nested

Nested is a "simulation of everything" by Orteil. True to its name, the site consists of a series of infinitely nested objects (presented in the form of tabs). Users can open an object tab to view all the objects it contains; once the subatomic level is reached and the smallest particle (a quark) opened, the cycle resets as the quark contains an entirely new universe within it. According to the developer, all elements inside Nested are randomised from a possible pool of objects its parent can contain.

To me, this web experiment is an example of how uncertainty, a large number of choices and an intriguing premise are just as important to an interactive experience. It is easy to get lost in the large number of interactive tabs and explore different objects contained within this randomly generated universe, even though the code behind the page is relatively simple. Nested reminds me that interactive media can be interesting without being complex—it just needs to compel the user.

https://orteil.dashnet.org/nested

nonsense laboratory

Nonsense Laboratory is a web experiment that allows users to play around with words and their pronunciations. Through a variety of machine learning-assisted tools on the page, users can enter a phrase and alter it using different parameters to receive a different spelling that results in the slight mutation of the word. With enough modification, the output will become unrecognisable from the original, thus becoming "nonsense".

As someone interested in linguistics, this website is very interesting to play around with in how it simulates linguistic drift and explains how spellings change over time. The wide number of variables I can alter provide me with plenty of ways to modify words and create my own nonsensical speech. Beyond being an enjoyable experience, this website helps introduce the basics of linguistic principles in an accessible way.

https://artsexperiments.withgoogle.com/nonsense-laboratory/

infinite craft

Inspired by games such as Little Alchemy, Infinite Craft enhances the concept by using generative AI. The user starts off with the four classical elements, which can be combined together to create new words. The use of AI means that words can be combined infinitely with no restrictions. The game encourages users to create bizarre phrases by combining increasingly complex things, with an indicator if they are the first person to discover a specific word.

The use of generative AI in gameplay has been a controversial topic, with its detractors saying that a curated pool of content is better than being able to infinitely generate low-quality elements. The game embraces this inherent downside of generative AI by incentivising the discovery of nonsensical items, which fits the theme of this subject.

https://neal.fun/infinite-craft/

blank stare

Blank Stare is a series of laser etchings on watercolour paper by artist Laura Splan. The etchings are waveforms created from electroencephalogram (EEG) measurements of her brain while she stared at a piece of paper for 60 seconds, measuring her attention level.

This work fascinates me as it fits into the "synaesthesia" portion of this subject, directly translating brain activity in the form of an artwork instead of simply visualising a sense. The circular eyelike etchings remind me of Jeremy Bentham's essay on the Panopticon, since it measures the artist's undivided attention on an object.

https://www.laurasplan.com/projects/blank-stare

dial-a-poem

Dial-a-Poem is a phone service created in 1968 by John Giorno. This service allowed its users to call a number and listen to a random poem from a selection of writers. The poetry included countercultural content including those celebrating queer love and Black Panther speeches; as a result, the original service was shut down in 1970. There have been several iterations of Dial-a-Poem across the years.

Though it does not resemble computing as we know today, this service is notable as an early example of art expressed through interactions between humans and electronic devices. In Giorno's words, “the voice was the poet, the words were the poem, and the telephone was the venue”. The experience of hearing a poem read to you is an intimate one, as if the poet is confessing their deepest thoughts to you. This intimacy contrasts with the physical distance of a phone call between two locations, leading to a novel experience for the time.

https://www.sfmoma.org/read/john-giornos-dial-a-poem-still-brings-poetry-to-the-masses/

aguaviva

Aguaviva is an installation created by Thomas Marcusson. It consists of a single jellyfish in a dome, whose movements are tracked by a camera and translated into randomly generated numbers. The work juxtaposes the spontaneous movements of a living organism with the ultimately semipredictable computer algorithms that generate random numbers, showing how living beings can be better at machines at some tasks.

The installation interests me due to how a nonhuman organism is interacting with a computer; in this case, being part of a process for generating random numbers. Though we often assume that computer interaction can only be performed by humans or trained animals, this installation could not exist without the jellyfish's participation. Aguaviva causes me to question my assumptions of what interaction with computers looks like.

https://thomasmarcusson.com/aguaviva.html

heterobota

Created by Agnieszka Pilat, Heterobota consists of three dog robots from Boston Dynamics, which are provided with a canvas on the wall and some painting tools. Vistors to the installation can observe the robots in their enclosure as they go about their daily routines and create their own art. The art, while technically rudimentary, is made semi-autonomously by the robots without any human input.

According to the artist, the work questions our threshold for machines to exist outside of their designated functions. In my opinion, this theme is particularly emphasized through the specific robots used in the installation; these robot dogs are used by police, and are capable of causing serious injury to a human. They have been widely criticised for threatening civil liberties. I find the contrast between the violence of their intended purpose and the benign act of creating marks on a canvas compelling, as it shows how these robots can go beyond their intended purpose.

https://www.ngv.vic.gov.au/program/heterobota-by-agnieszka-pilat/
